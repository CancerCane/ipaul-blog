I hear this a lot from fellow vets: "I'm not a tech person." I used to say the same thing.

## The military already taught you the hard parts

Think about what the military drills into you:

- **Problem solving under pressure** — That's debugging.
- **Following procedures exactly** — That's writing code.
- **Adapting when the plan falls apart** — That's every software project ever.
- **Working in teams with clear communication** — That's collaborative development.

The hard part of tech isn't the syntax. It's the discipline to keep going when things don't work. You already have that.

## The practical case

Tech jobs pay well and many are remote. For a veteran transitioning out, that flexibility matters. You can live where you want, work on your schedule (mostly), and build something tangible every day.

## Where to start

My honest recommendation:

1. **Start with HTML/CSS** — Build a simple website. See it in the browser. Feel the win.
2. **Add JavaScript** — Make things interactive. This is where it gets fun.
3. **Pick a framework** — React, Jekyll, whatever. Just pick one and build a real project.
4. **Use free resources** — freeCodeCamp, The Odin Project, YouTube. The information is out there.

The GI Bill covers coding bootcamps too. Don't sleep on that.

## Final thought

You already survived harder things than a compiler error. You've got this.
